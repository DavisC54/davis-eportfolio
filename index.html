Cover Letter

Welcome to my ENC 1102 portfolio. I came into this class as a programmer who saw writing courses as something to get through efficiently, not as a place I’d build anything I cared about. Now in the final days of this semester I have a research project that I am very proud of, an analysis of how MrBeast-style “$1 vs $X” YouTube thumbnails and titles are designed to grab a teen audience, and everything in between that shows the process that got me here. My argument for this portfolio is simple: across the semester my writing moved from guessing at what research is to actually doing it, and the six course outcomes are the clearest way to see that change. The artifacts I’ve chosen aren’t just proof I finished the assignments; lined up, they trace a research process, from a first messy question, to sources, to a coding method, to a drafted and revised paper, and each stage maps onto an outcome I can point to and show.

The course outcome for Generating Inquiry asks students to “generate and explore genuine lines of inquiry related to writing, language, literacy, and/or rhetoric,” and the artifact that shows me learning what that means is my research question, which sits in the Research Project section next to the genre analysis that started it. My first version asked how effective the MrBeast clickbait style was at raising viewership across age groups. Dr. Barnickel pointed out that this wasn’t really researchable for the course, I was chasing an outcome I had no way to measure. I reworked it into “how do the rhetorical and visual features of MrBeast-style ‘$1 vs $X’ YouTube thumbnails and titles work to capture the attention of American teens between 2019 and 2025?” That swap, from a question about effects to a question about the text itself, is the whole outcome in one move: a genuine line of inquiry is one where the answer isn’t already baked in and where I can actually go look. I kept the weak first version in my portfolio on purpose, because the distance between those two questions is the clearest evidence I have that I learned this.

Information Literacy, the outcome about evaluating “relevance, credibility, and ethics when gathering, analyzing, and presenting primary and secondary source materials,” is the one my annotated bibliography demonstrates most directly. Early on I basically treated anything on the internet as usable. Building the bibliography pushed me past that. I pulled eight sources through UCF’s library databases, and the work wasn’t just finding them, it was sorting real peer-reviewed articles from books, dissertations, and reference entries, then asking of each one what the author’s angle was and how it connected to what everyone else had said, instead of only whether it was “trustworthy.” The ethics half showed up in the citation discipline it took to present those sources honestly. Presenting sources accurately turned out to be as much a part of this outcome as picking good ones.

Research Genre Production asks for writing that shows I can “navigate choices and constraints in a variety of public and/or academic research genres that matter to specific communities.” My portfolio is basically a tour of those genres: reading responses, a genre analysis, the annotated bibliography, a coding-practice assignment, claim drafts, a multimodal discussion post, and the full research paper. Each one runs on different rules. The reading responses are loose and exploratory; the annotated bibliography is rigid, with strict citation formatting and a fixed summary-then-connection structure; the research paper, “A Dollar Against a Fortune,” had to organize eight sources into three clusters and hold an academic argument balancing and utilizing these sources. The hardest constraint was one specific to my project: Dr. Barnickel kept flagging “effectiveness language,” any time I claimed the format “works” or was “perfected to grab” attention. Staying in what I started calling the rhetorical-design lane, describing what the thumbnails are built to do rather than claiming what they achieve, shaped almost every sentence of that paper. Learning to write inside that limit is the clearest sign I can navigate a genre’s rules instead of just filling in a template.

Multiple Ways of Writing is about purposefully integrating “multimodality, multiple languages, and/or multiliteracies into writing products to support their goals,” and it runs through more of my work than I expected when I first defined it as just not being a robot and tossing in an image. My intro discussion post made the point early, it was built around a meme, and I had to think about what an image could say that a paragraph couldn’t. The research project takes that further, because the whole object of study is multimodal: I’m analyzing how a thumbnail’s image and a title’s text combine to make meaning, and my appendix marks up each thumbnail with a color key so a reader can see the conventions I’m naming. And the portfolio you’re reading is itself the multimodal product, where the layout, the navigation, and the writing all have to work together. The progress here is that I stopped treating modes as decoration and started choosing them for what they actually do.

Contributing Knowledge, or drawing “conclusions based on analysis and interpretation of primary evidence” and placing that work “in conversation with other source materials,” is the outcome my research paper exists to demonstrate. My primary evidence is the thumbnails and titles themselves, four of them, coded against a consistent set of criteria I built in my coding-practice assignment. The conclusions are mine: that the format runs on a small core of conventions, a fixed price-contrast title formula, an exaggerated surprised face, and a bright, gold-saturated version of an expensive-looking product. Then I put that in conversation with James Porter’s idea of iterability and discourse communities, applying it to a genre Porter never wrote about, and with the visual-rhetoric sources from my bibliography. That’s the part that finally felt like contributing instead of summarizing, taking someone’s framework and using it to say something about evidence they never looked at.

Revision, the outcome about acting “with intention on feedback from readers when drafting, revising, and editing,” is the one I changed my mind about the most. I used to think revision meant fixing typos and tightening sentences. This semester taught me it can happen at the level of a whole idea. The clearest case is the research-question rewrite, that wasn’t editing, it was rethinking the entire project after Dr. Barnickel’s feedback. In my coding-practice assignment, Dr. Barnickel pointed out that I was making claims without showing evidence, so I went back, expanded my sample from three thumbnails to four, and marked up the actual images so every present-or-absent call had documented proof behind it. And late in the project, when I added a fourth creator and two of the four turned out to share the same collective, my earlier claim that they were “unrelated creators” stopped being true; instead of deleting it I reframed it into a stronger point about a convention that travels both across strangers and inside a group. Each of those was me negotiating a difference, between what I’d written and what the evidence or the feedback was telling me, and choosing to act on it on purpose. My drafts keep the before and after so that negotiation stays visible.

Looking back, the through-line of this portfolio is that I stopped treating research as something you assert and started treating it as something you build and defend. That isn’t a writing-class-only skill. I’m a programmer, and the habits this project drilled, asking a question I can actually investigate, checking my sources before I trust them, staying honest about what my evidence does and doesn’t show, and revising the whole structure when something underneath it changes, are the same habits that separate a real technical write-up, design doc, or postmortem from a pile of claims. Whether I end up documenting a system, writing up a game-development project, or making a case to a team, I’ll be doing a version of what I did here. The MrBeast thumbnails were the excuse; learning to think and write like this was the point.

About Me

I’m Davis Cerniglia, a Digital Media major at UCF on the Game Design programming track. Most of what I do sits somewhere between code and design, building games, writing the scripts that run them, and figuring out why they break. That’s pretty much the lens I bring to everything, including this class.

As a writer I work the same way I do on a build: a lot of drive and not much patience for leaving things half-finished. I’d rather redo something five times until it actually holds than turn in a version that just looks done. I came into ENC 1102 expecting to write like a programmer, fast and to the point, and I left realizing research writing rewards the same stubbornness I put into debugging, ask a real question, check it against evidence, and be willing to rip up the structure when something underneath it changes.

Outside of class I’m into sports and video games, which is honestly part of why this project clicked for me. Looking at how YouTube thumbnails are built to grab attention is right where the stuff I already watch meets the design side I care about.

I picked MrBeast-style “$1 vs $X” thumbnails because I actually want to start a YouTube channel someday, so figuring out how big creators grab attention wasn’t just an assignment to me. My first research question asked how effective these thumbnails are at raising viewership. Annotated Bibliography

Information LiteracyResearch Genre ProductionRevision

This is the assignment that made me actually evaluate sources instead of just grabbing whatever looked useful. I pulled eight sources and wrote a summary and a connection for each one, all tied back to my question. Most of Dr. Barnickel’s notes were about MLA stuff I’d missed, italicizing the journal titles, double-spacing the whole thing, and labeling what kind of source each one was. Going back and marking each one as scholarly and peer-reviewed, or as course material for the textbook, made me think about whether a source was actually credible and not just whether it backed me up. Honestly the formatting fixes mattered more than I figured, getting the genre right is part of the work.

Data Coding Assignment

Contributing KnowledgeInformation LiteracyRevision

This is where the project started making findings of its own instead of leaning on other people’s research. My first version set up three data points but I only actually coded one, the title formula, across three videos. Dr. Barnickel said it was a good start but I needed real examples and screenshots to back up what I was claiming. So I went back, added a fourth video, coded all three data points, and marked up the actual thumbnails so every yes or no had proof behind it. It ended up changing what I found, too. I figured the cheap-vs-expensive split would be the main thing, but once I coded all four it only showed up in half of them, while the shocked face and gold product were in every single one.

Final Research Paper: “A Dollar Against a Fortune”

Contributing KnowledgeResearch Genre ProductionMultiple Ways of Writing

This is the one everything else was building toward. It pulls together my research question, the eight sources from my bibliography, and the coding method from the data assignment into a single argument: that the “$1 vs $X” thumbnail works like a genre, with a core of conventions every creator uses and a thin optional layer some of them drop. Writing it meant actually holding an argument across an intro, lit review, methods, results, and discussion, which is the full shape of a research paper. The hardest part was staying in the lane Dr. Barnickel kept pushing me toward, describing what the thumbnails are built to do instead of claiming what they pull off. It’s also the most multimodal thing I made, since the whole point is how the image and the title work together, and the coded appendix lets you see the conventions instead of just taking my word for it.

Additional Artifacts

Two more pieces from across the semester. Same setup: original, feedback, and revised.

Making a Claim

Contributing KnowledgeRevisionInformation Literacy

This was the first time I actually committed to an argument instead of just describing what I saw. My claim was that the “$1 vs $X” style isn’t one creator’s thing, it’s a shared convention, and that the price gap does more of the work than whatever object is in the video. Dr. Barnickel said the claim was good but I was just asserting it with no evidence, and that I needed documented proof for every claim I make. Revising it taught me a claim isn’t done when it sounds right, it’s done when someone can actually check it. I added the title table and the real thumbnails so you can see the pattern instead of just trusting me, and kept the Porter connection so it’s tied back to the research.

Matching Research Questions to Primary Research

Information LiteracyGenerating InquiryRevision

This was an early exercise where I had to match different research questions to the methods that would actually answer them. It’s basically where I started picking up the language of research design, that things like observation, textual analysis, and archival work aren’t interchangeable and each fits a certain kind of question. Dr. Barnickel’s notes made me get specific, she asked what kind of textual analysis I meant on one of them, and pointed out that something I’d called “working with visuals” was really archival research. Fixing those made me actually name my methods instead of throwing them in broad buckets, which is the same thing I had to do later when I built my own coding method.
